Which Variety Should I Plant?

There's no real "best" variety among the common, easy-to-source options. Just two personal preferences to sort out, once you know which type is right for where you live.

Zones & Chill Hours

Two simple checks, easy to overthink

Ideal growing area, with the least to think about: the Midwest and Southeast. Elsewhere, blueberries are still genuinely doable — just a few more easy-to-fix factors to account for, not a fundamentally different process. Northern Highbush is this guide's standard recommendation because most readers will be somewhere it's the straightforwardly correct choice — not because it's universally the right answer everywhere.

Question 1

Cold-Hardy Enough?

Northern Highbush is the most cold-hardy type — safe almost anywhere blueberries make sense at all. Rabbiteye and Southern Highbush are less cold-hardy; plant them outside their safe zone and the plant may survive while failing to fruit, or suffer real winter dieback.

Question 2

Enough Winter Chill?

Only a real concern in genuinely mild-winter climates (Deep South, parts of the Pacific Coast) trying to grow a high-chill Northern Highbush (800–1100 hrs). Not enough chill means delayed, erratic flowering — a completely different failure mode than cold damage, easy to confuse with it.

An honest caveat specifically for zone 3

The berm method this whole guide is built around is about as close to guaranteed as this guide gets for zones 4 through 10 — that range is genuinely well-covered. Zone 3 specifically is the one real exception, because true winter extreme (-30°F to -40°F+) is a different problem than anything else this guide has been tested against. Zone 2 isn't really a further concern beyond that — it's roughly where plant life in general starts to become unrecognizable as anything most people would call a garden plant, blueberries included.

The Half-High varieties above are real and confirmed. What isn't confirmed is whether a raised berm interacts well with the snow-cover insulation strategy Half-High breeding specifically depends on — a different protective mechanism than anything else in this guide relies on. Zone 3 growers should treat the variety recommendations as solid, and the berm/planting-method guidance as a reasonable starting point rather than a confirmed answer — worth checking with a local extension office or an experienced zone 3 grower before assuming the method translates directly from zones 4-10 down to true zone 3 conditions.

The simple version, without the tool

In most of the US, including anywhere with a real winter, Northern Highbush at its typical chill requirement is fine and matches what this guide already recommends — don't overthink it further.

Rabbiteye is the recommended home-grower choice zones 8 and 9 — same reasoning as everywhere else in this guide: more forgiving, more disease-resistant, and a wider margin for error than Southern Highbush. Rabbiteye's real ceiling is zone 9, though — it has its own chill-hour floor (typically 350+ hours depending on cultivar), and in the very warmest zone 9 pockets and zone 10, that minimum stops being reliably met. That's specifically when Southern Highbush becomes necessary — not a general upgrade over Rabbiteye, just the answer once Rabbiteye's own chill requirement genuinely can't be satisfied.

Worth knowing if SHB is what's required for you: it's genuinely the more finicky type to grow, less tolerant of minor issues that Rabbiteye or Northern Highbush would shrug off — confirmed earlier in this guide (more disease-prone, narrower margin for error, requires closer to ideal conditions). This isn't meant to discourage anyone — if you're in a climate where SHB is the only real option, there isn't an alternative anyway. Just go in knowing it asks for a bit more attention than the other two types.

One useful distinction if you're near the cooler edge of SHB's range: the risk usually isn't the plant dying, it's inconsistent fruiting. Same underlying pattern as the rabbiteye grow-vs-fruit distinction covered earlier, different mechanism — SHB blooms very early in the year by design (that's how it achieves such low chill requirements), so a cooler-than-ideal zone means repeated late frosts damaging the flowers before fruit sets, not the bush itself failing to survive. Frost protection during bloom (row cover, frost cloth) is the real fix if pushing the cooler edge of a variety's range.

The Actual Decision

Simpler than it sounds

When do you want to pick?

Earlier = cooler weather, less pest pressure. Later = more heat and bugs to deal with while picking — not worth it for most home growers. Berry size is a variety trait, not a season one; plenty of late-season varieties are just as large as early ones.

What berry size do you prefer?

Minor variety-to-variety differences; most common varieties taste fine. Medium/large is the common default most people want.

Pollination — required info, varies by type

Northern Highbush (and Half-High): optional, but ideal. Self-fertile — a single variety alone will fruit fine. A second variety boosts yield and berry size 30-40%, and is basically free to add since NHB varieties all bloom within about a week of each other regardless of when they ripen. No strict compatibility rules like fruit trees have.

Rabbiteye: required, not optional. Effectively self-sterile — a single rabbiteye variety planted alone will bloom but largely won't set fruit. Two or more different rabbiteye varieties with overlapping bloom times are a real requirement here, not a yield bonus.

Southern Highbush: required for most varieties, with a few named exceptions. Unlike NHB, SHB self-fertility is cultivar-dependent, not a given. Most SHB varieties need a partner just like rabbiteye does. Sunshine Blue, O'Neal, and Sharpblue are confirmed partially self-fertile, though even these still produce better with a second variety. Sunshine Blue is the most consistently easy of the three — some of the others' zone/hardiness claims are more contested and depend on active care (frost protection during bloom, etc.) than Sunshine Blue's.

How pollination actually happens, and the one thing outside your control. Blueberries aren't wind-pollinated — the pollen is too heavy and sticky. It takes an insect. Bumblebees and a few native specialist bees are especially good at it thanks to "buzz pollination" (vibrating the flower to shake pollen loose), something honeybees can't technically do — though honeybees still manage it well enough that they're what most commercial farms actually rely on. The real risk for a home grower isn't which bee shows up, it's whether any pollinator is active during the bloom window at all — bees of every kind forage less in cold, windy, or overcast weather, and a stretch of bad weather right at bloom time can mean poor fruit set even on a self-fertile variety that's otherwise doing everything right.

A note on the trademarked varieties you'll see advertised

Proven Winners' "Bushel and Berry" line — Peach Sorbet®, Pink Icing®, Blueberry Glaze™, Jelly Bean®, Blueberry Buckle®, Perpetua® (notable for two harvests per season) — represents genuine, serious breeding work, bred primarily for compact size and ornamental foliage suited to container/patio growing. These are legitimately nice plants — it's just not what this guide is built around.

Worth knowing: the same appealing traits often show up in older, non-trademarked varieties too, without the markup or branding. Sweetheart, for example, is a plain Northern Highbush variety that also produces a second, smaller harvest in fall if the season stays mild — the same "two harvests" idea Perpetua is marketed around. The autumn crop isn't really substantial enough to count as a meaningful second harvest — a nice bonus, not a real yield boost. Genuinely a case of a trait that sounds better than it functions, trademarked or not.

The real, relevant difference for this guide isn't trademarked vs. not — it's that these varieties are specifically suited to container growing, and that's a different set of tradeoffs than the berm-planting approach here:

  • More frequent, attentive watering than a berm needs. Fixable with a timer, but there's real risk in letting a day slip the way a berm planting can tolerate.
  • Automating it well takes more setup than a drip line across a berm — doable, just more work.
  • Nutrients deplete faster as water runs through and out. Fixable with more frequent feeding.
  • A real catch-22 with sun exposure — full sun is needed to fruit well, but it bakes the root zone in a container. Fixable with a bigger or light-colored pot.
  • Containers are heavy and hard to move once a bush reaches any real size — repositioning for winter protection isn't the simple task it might sound like.
  • Real gap in winter protection, and not a small one. A berm sits within enough surrounding earth mass to only be marginally less protected than growing directly in the ground. A container has none of that buffer — roots are genuinely exposed to ambient air temperature on all sides, with no earth mass moderating the swings at all.

None of this is impossible — it's just meaningfully more ongoing work than a berm planting ever asks for. Container berries depend on you to live. In-ground/berm-planted bushes do not. These varieties were bred to fill a real demand for patio/small-space growing — they're not bad plants. This guide is written for the easier path; if containers are still what's wanted, it can be done — just go in knowing what it actually takes.
